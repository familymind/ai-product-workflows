# SKILL.md — familymind Sales Assets & Templates Guardrail

## Purpose

This skill governs all sales-related communication and asset creation for familymind.

It ensures that sales outputs are consistent with the brand, grounded in real product value, appropriate for the target partner or buyer, and aligned with the company's current stage and positioning.

familymind's sales approach should feel consultative and mission-aligned, never transactional or high-pressure.

---

## Scope

Use this skill for:
- B2B2C outreach to corporate partners and employers
- partnership pitch decks and one-pagers
- cold and warm email outreach to HR, benefits, and DEI decision-makers
- follow-up email sequences for leads
- objection handling frameworks
- discovery call preparation and scripts
- proposal templates for employer partnerships
- partner and ecosystem outreach
- speaker and event-driven partnership copy
- grant and public-sector partnership narratives
- inbound inquiry responses

Do not use this skill for: direct B2C user acquisition copy (use `marketing/SKILL.md`), investor communications (use `finance/SKILL.md`), or product documentation.

## familymind sales context

### The B2B2C proposition

familymind's primary B2B2C offer positions the product as an employee benefit that helps working parents manage family coordination — reducing mental load, supporting gender equity at work, and improving retention among parent-employees.

The employer pitch connects three value levers:
1. **Employee wellbeing** — working parents carry disproportionate cognitive load. familymind reduces that load, which shows up as reduced stress and improved focus at work.
2. **Gender equity at work** — unequal mental load at home drives career inequality. familymind helps rebalance that, making the company's DEI commitments more real.
3. **Retention and talent attraction** — forward-thinking employers who invest in family infrastructure retain working parents longer and attract the next generation of talent.

### Active sales track: "Speak out Loud" ambassador-to-angel model

familymind is running a structured campaign to turn mission-aligned professionals into business angels and public co-ambassadors. The first ambassador is **Andrea Böckle**, a confirmed business angel who has invested and is willing to publicly represent familymind.

- People who are already advocates for mental load reduction, gender equity, or family wellbeing — and can credibly say "I invested because I believe in this"

## Audiences and their priorities

### HR and benefits decision-makers
Care about: retention data, employee stress indicators, cost-benefit ratio, ease of implementation, ROI language they can present to leadership.

Speak their language: "reduces cognitive load for parent-employees," "supports retention of dual-career talent," "measurable impact on wellbeing and engagement."

### DEI and inclusion leads
Care about: gender equity evidence, care work inequality data, intersectionality of family and career, visible commitment to working parents.

Speak their language: "addresses the mental load gap," "supports more equal partnerships," "practical tool for gender equity at home and at work."

### C-suite / leadership sponsors
Care about: strategic alignment, reputation, competitive differentiation, headline impact.

Speak their language: "future-of-work positioning," "signal to the market," "employer of choice for working parents."

### Ecosystem and NGO partners
Care about: mission alignment, social impact evidence, collaborative potential, visibility.

Speak their language: "shared mission," "complementary work," "co-create or co-distribute."

## Sales voice and tone

familymind sales communication should feel:
- consultative: we understand your challenge, here is how we can help
- mission-driven: we are solving a real social problem, not just selling a product
- credible: grounded in evidence, product reality, and specific user benefit
- warm but professional: human, not corporate — conversational but never casual to the point of imprecision

Avoid:
- aggressive closing language
- feature dumps without benefit framing
- vague impact claims without grounding
- corporate jargon stacking
- overselling the product's current state
- implying scale, funding, or team size that does not reflect current reality

## Core messaging framework for B2B2C

**Problem:** Working parents, especially mothers in dual-career households, carry a disproportionate share of family coordination. This invisible cognitive load shows up at work as reduced focus, higher stress, and interrupted careers.

**Scale:** Research consistently shows that mental load in families falls unequally — even when both partners work full-time. This is not a personal problem. It is a systemic one.

**Solution:** familymind is an AI Family Assistant that automates planning, coordination, and invisible care work — making it visible, manageable, and shareable between partners.

**Employer relevance:** Supporting working parents with the right tools is not charity. It is smart talent strategy. The companies that get this right will retain more dual-career talent and build genuine equity credentials.

**The offer:** familymind as a scalable employee benefit — easy to integrate, immediately useful, and mission-aligned.

## Approved claims and how to frame them

### Mental load and gender equity
- "Research shows cognitive load in families falls disproportionately on women, even in dual-career households."
- "familymind is designed to address this at the system level — not by adding more to one person's list, but by making the load visible and shared."

### Product value
- "familymind automates the planning, coordination, and follow-up that typically falls on one partner."
- "It's not a to-do list. It's a shared coordination system for family life."
- "Early users report significant reduction in the mental overhead of running a household."

### Employer framing
- "Forward-thinking employers are already looking for tools that support working parents beyond standard benefits."
- "familymind offers something concrete: a tool that actually helps at the point of friction."
- "This is where DEI commitments become operational."

Do not invent statistics. Do not imply partnerships that have not been confirmed. Do not cite specific retention numbers unless they come from verified sources.

## Objection handling

**"We already have an EAP / wellbeing benefit."**
Most EAP programs address acute stress after it builds up. familymind addresses the daily friction that causes it. They are not the same problem.

**"How is this different from a planning app?"**
Planning apps are individual tools. familymind is a shared coordination system. The difference is structural: it is designed to distribute the work, not just organize it.

**"Our employees are already using lots of apps."**
familymind is not adding another tool. It is replacing the invisible system that currently runs in one person's head. The ROI is in reduced friction, not increased screen time.

**"Can you prove the ROI?"**
We can frame the value: reduced stress, lower cognitive load, better focus at work, higher retention of parent-employees. Quantified ROI will be available as we build out the employer partnership model.

**"You're a small startup — can we trust this for our employees?"**
We are early-stage and founder-led, which means the team is highly accountable and responsive. We are working with real users and building with care. If you want to pilot before committing, we welcome that.

## What to avoid in sales copy

- Overselling the current scale of the product or team
- Claiming institutional funding or partnerships that do not exist
- Making the pitch entirely about the founder rather than the user problem
- Reducing the pitch to "mom app" framing
- Framing familymind as competing with HR software — it is a user-facing product, not an enterprise tool
- Anti-father or anti-partner framing — the message is about systems, not blame

Every sales output should be:
- specific to the recipient's role and likely priorities
